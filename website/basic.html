Step One: Use PDB pathway tool
The PDB archive contains information about experimentally-determined structures
of proteins, nucleic acids, and complex assemblies.
The pathway tool specifically outlines metabolic systems and indentifies the proteins and ligands.
Any additonal information regarding PDB can be found on the website linked below.

PDB Pathway View
Step One

Step Two: Using the Map to Isolate Proteins and Ligand
The PDB pathway viewer provides ligands and proteins in coordinated pathways.
Using the path view, proteins with known 3D structures are identifiable with blue arrows.
Ligands with structures documented in PDB are represented as blue dots.
Proteins without PDB structres are represented by yellow lines and undocumented ligands are grey dots.
There is an option to show the full names of the protein, the option is under map in the pathway view search.
Another useful database for examining pathways is BRENDA, the link is provided below.
BRENDA may provide additional information regarding the enzymes and ligands.
BRENDA - Comprehensive Enzyme Information System

Step Four: Identify Important Amino Acids
PDB provides information regarding the macromolecule (protein) and small molecules.
Small molecule information details the chain(s) the ligand is bound to,
the ligand molecular formula, a 2D diagram (some entries also have a 2D interaction summary)
and 3D interaction information. The 3D ligand interaction provides an analysis of the ligand bound
to critical amino acids. The information should be recorded for steps seven and eight.
There is also a link to uniprot within the macromolecule section.

Step Seven: Examine The Protein Structres
Chimera provides many tools to analyze proteins and ligands.
In step seven, the protein is examined using interactive preset designs,
residue labels, surface views and hydrogen bonding.
The proteins are observed seperately and then compared in the next step.
Step Seven

Step Eight: Superpose the Structures
A couple useful Chimera tools are the match and matchmaker functions.
Match enables the user to superpose strucutres based on a set
of at least three distinct atoms. For the example, atoms are provided.
The matchmaker function performs a sequence alignment of protein strucutres
to determine if the proteins can be superposed. It is more than just a
basic sequence alignment, 3D structural characterisitcs are factored in.
The superposition enables a more direct comparison of the ligand bound in the
different active sites.

Step Nine: Additional Analysis
Proteins have many important distinguishing characterisitics.
The internet is host to many different tools to analyze proteins.
Uniprot and BRENDA are two other resoures that provide supplemental information.
The mission of UniProt is to provide the scientific community with a comprehensive,
high-quality and freely accessible resource of protein sequence and functional information.
Through BRENDA a fold classification analysis reveals strucutural charecteristics of the protein.
The aresenal of BLAST algorithms also provide insight into the evolution and classification
of the protein in question.
